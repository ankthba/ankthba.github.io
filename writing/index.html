Short answers

What is the most significant challenge that society faces today?

Think about the laptop before you, the phone in your pocket, the plane above, and the hospital a mile away from you. They're all powered by transistors. However, they can't shrink beyond atomic limits. As the need for computing forever grows, society's greatest challenge is working around this physical boundary.

How did you spend your last two summers?

The faint red glow of a resistor on my screen dimly lit the room as I brought a PCB to life. Shaping code into creation and soldering fragile components, each moment felt electric. I spent my summers turning silly ideas into reality, bringing creativity into the precision of engineering.

What historical moment or event do you wish you could have witnessed?

There's something alluring about WWDC 2009: Federighi's first keynote, perfecting MacOS in Snow Leopard. To the untrained eye, everything was shrouded in mystery. But deep within, a network of groundbreaking architecture lay. That's my fork of innovation: subtle and obsessive with details most never see, but those that change everything.

Briefly elaborate on one of your extracurricular activities, a job you hold, or responsibilities you have for your family.

I can still remember my tears as a survivor told me he wished my project existed earlier. I built OCRadar to fight hidden battles, a way to use my love for coding and technology to detect oral cancer before it's missed. Innovation matters most when it brings purpose to others.

List five things that are important to you.

  • Curiosity: getting lost in the hum of planes overhead, driving me to build ideas.
  • Late-night coffee with friends: fuel for collaboration and laughter.
  • My camera: my portal for expression.
  • The clicks of my laptop: my path from imagination to creation.
  • Walks in nature: a reminder to breathe and see perspective.
Essays

The Stanford community is deeply curious and driven to learn in and out of the classroom. Reflect on an idea or experience that makes you genuinely excited about learning.

There's a special kind of excitement that comes from being completely lost in a physics textbook, the wires in my brain tangled in equation after equation. For days, I was stuck in the conceptual struggle. Things just wouldn't click. Instead of stepping back, I shot forward, curiosity leading me through the chapters. Suddenly, the seemingly complex abstractions vanished as I saw how electric potential shapes voltage and current to power entire systems. I realized that all the sockets, circuits, and devices I have used for years were real-life proof of these hidden laws.

My excitement couldn't be controlled. I spent hours reading, sketching diagrams, and solving problems. Not for a grade or a quiz, but purely for the joy in understanding the invisible architectures that power the world. One question led to another. How do these concepts apply to circuits? How do electrons flow in diverse networks? This induced wonder, transforming the idea of learning from an endless cycle of work into an adventure.

What began as confusion in complex topics became my spark for exploring; one that charges me to dig deep into every topic I come across, no matter how daunting. That day, Physics E&M taught me the excitement in chasing understanding; a spark that pushes me through classes, labs, and everywhere I learn. This is the same spark that drives me to Stanford.

Please describe what aspects of your life experiences, interests and character would help you make a distinctive contribution as an undergraduate to Stanford University.

Stanford will never get bored of me. It's more than just me wiring complex robots, mentoring future generations of STEM students, and shaping the future of healthcare with AI. It's also because I truly love bringing people together for midnight coding sessions, hikes at sunrise, and spontaneous tech show visits that start with curiosity and end with newfound friends.

The best innovations happen when everyone is heard. Whether that's developing tools to improve someone's mobility or making sure everyone has a voice in a robotics project. My favorite moments aren't achievements, they're the times when I hear, "this changed my life." I never dreamed that I would have such an impact, especially at such a young age.

The world I bring is imaginative, intellectual, and always built on empathy. I've photographed aviation ever since I first held a camera, seeing a story behind every bleak airport terminal. I'm a relentless inventor whose projects mean the most when it helps even a single patient. I'm sociable with everyone, always up for coffee runs during midterm season or offering tech support at any hour of the day.

At Stanford, I'll bring my passions, curiosity, and commitment for what I love. I want to create spaces where people feel welcomed and innovation thrives. Whether in the Student Space Initiative lab, hiking the Dish, or fountain hopping, I want to add to Stanford's culture: one where invention flourishes and big ideas are encouraged. This contribution will never dry in the California sun.

Deferral update

Reflect on your current year studies. What excites you about your learning and why? (125 words)

I keep finding the same pattern in every topic I approach. Renaissance perspective in art history mirrors the field lines I trace in Magnetism. In comparative government, endless constitutions and bureaucratics somehow reflect the way All the King's Men breaks down corruption through narrative. Even economics shows me where ethics cross with mathematics. Why? Because I'm not learning subjects anymore. I've discovered that every discipline is a dialect of the same language: a path through making comparisons, building off prior knowledge, and drawing parallels. Knowledge is versatile, intriguing, and, most of all, always unified. Using this knowledge, I know that daunting topics don't intimidate me anymore; they all have underlying themes connecting back to something I've already learned and loved.

Tell us about an accomplishment you are proud of that occurred after you submitted your application. (125 words)

I'm building an operating system because the future is trapped in the 1970s. Unix philosophy: files, processes, and isolation. What was once elegant has become a cage. AI agents need fluid permissions, semantic reasoning, and systems that understand rather than simply execute.

I descended to the hardware level, writing Rust bootloaders whispering directly with hardware, sculpted memory managers from raw address space, and designed schedulers that think with consciousness, not clockwork. The kernel doesn't follow rigid hierarchies; it negotiates intent.

The first "Hello World" rendered to the screen felt amazing, it was philosophy compiled to silicon. Legacy architectures exist not because alternatives are impossible, but because most don't question the foundations.

The future is always waiting; I just had to write it myself.

How have you grown since you submitted your application? Are there any insights or realizations that have changed how you approach the world? (125 words)

I approached Economics casually, expecting intellectual tourism. Instead, I was met with theoretical density, problem sets like complex debates, and models that went against intuitions. My typical velocity stalled. Every assumption I built about my capacity froze.

In that pressure, something transformed.

Difficulty stopped acting as the enemy. Instead, it became the friction that shows I'm touching something real. The "never give up" cliches crystallized into an appetite for the impossible. I wasn't enduring the suffering, but rather becoming alive as a result of it.

When I face challenges, I'm no longer looking for the light at the end of the tunnel. I find a home in the unresolved, and a joy in what nearly breaks me. Resilience isn't surviving difficulty, it's craving it.
